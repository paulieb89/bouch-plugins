# Page-Specific Notes

Guidance for each main page on bouch.dev. Use alongside `voice-rules.md`.

---

## Homepage Hero

Hook with the problem, not the solution. The reader should think "that's me" before they read the second sentence.

Good pattern: [common frustrating situation] + [honest observation] + [what's actually available]

Bad pattern: "AI is changing everything. Here's how to get ahead."

The hero is not the place to explain the service. It is the place to make the reader feel recognised. They came here sceptical. The first sentence needs to stop them leaving.

---

## Services Page

Lead with the customer question, not the service name. "We bought tools but nobody uses them" is a customer question. "Mode 2 Training" is a service name. The customer does not know what Mode 2 Training means, but they know exactly what "tools nobody uses" feels like.

Structure: customer question as headline, then what the service actually is, then what happens in it, then who it is for.

Avoid: service names as headlines, internal jargon, abstract outcomes like "alignment" or "capability building."

---

## Tools / Products Page

State what the skill does in one sentence. Then the problem it solves. Features as bullet points. No hype.

The reader is a buyer who already knows roughly what they want. Give them the facts. If the skill searches Companies House, say it searches Companies House. Do not say it "unlocks insights from official registers."

---

## Work / Case Studies

Problem first, outcome second. Name the client type if not naming the client (e.g. "a property investment firm"). Use real numbers if available — hours saved, documents processed, cost of previous approach.

Avoid: "helped X achieve Y through our innovative approach," any framing that makes the consultant the hero rather than the outcome, vague qualitative results where quantitative ones exist.

---

## About Page

Paul's background should read like a CV entry, not a brand story. Credentials and projects. Deployed URLs where possible. No "passion for AI" language. No origin story framing. The reader wants to know if Paul can do the work, not why he finds it meaningful.
